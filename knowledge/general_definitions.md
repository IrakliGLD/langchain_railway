# General Definitions

Basic energy sector terminology for answering general conceptual questions.
Provides universal definitions with Georgia-specific context.

## Renewable Energy
**Definition:** Energy generated from natural sources that replenish faster than they are consumed. Main types include solar, wind, hydropower, geothermal, and biomass.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's electricity is predominantly renewable, with hydropower providing over 80% of domestic generation. Wind and solar capacity is growing under PPA and CfD support schemes. Key renewable sources: large HPPs (Enguri, Vardnili), small HPPs, and emerging wind/solar projects.

## Electricity Market
**Definition:** A system where electricity is bought and sold between generators, traders, suppliers, and consumers. Markets can include bilateral contracts, day-ahead trading, intraday trading, and balancing mechanisms.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia operates a hybrid market with: (1) Bilateral contracts between generators and suppliers, (2) Balancing market (since 2006) for residual supply/demand, (3) Day-ahead exchange (since July 2024). ESCO (Electricity System Commercial Operator) manages settlements.

## Balancing Market
**Definition:** A mechanism to balance real-time electricity supply and demand when actual consumption differs from scheduled/contracted amounts. Prices reflect the cost of last-resort supply adjustments.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's balancing market is the main price-setting mechanism. Balancing electricity price is a weighted average of all sources (regulated HPPs, deregulated hydro, thermal PPAs, renewable PPAs, imports). Summer prices are lower (hydro-dominated), winter prices higher (thermal/import-dominated).

## Tariff
**Definition:** A regulated price for electricity, set by government authorities. Tariffs can apply to generators (feed-in tariffs) or end consumers (retail tariffs).

**Georgia Context:** GNERC (Georgian National Energy and Water Supply Regulatory Commission) sets cost-plus tariffs for regulated generators including large HPPs (Enguri, Vardnili) and thermal plants (Gardabani TPP, old TPPs). Tariffs are denominated in GEL but adjusted for exchange rate changes.

## PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)
**Definition:** A long-term contract between an electricity generator and a buyer (utility, government, or corporate) guaranteeing purchase of electricity at agreed prices for a set period (typically 10-25 years).

**Georgia Context:** Georgia uses PPAs as a support scheme for new generation capacity. Renewable PPAs cover hydro, wind, and solar projects. Thermal PPAs exist for gas-fired plants. PPA prices are typically USD-indexed. PPAs provide revenue certainty for investors but create long-term price commitments.

## CfD (Contract for Difference)
**Definition:** A financial instrument where a generator receives (or pays back) the difference between a guaranteed 'strike price' and the market price. If market price is below strike price, generator receives a top-up payment; if above, generator pays back the difference.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia introduced CfD scheme in 2023 for new renewable projects through capacity auctions. First auction (2023) awarded ~170 MW to hydro, solar, and wind projects. CfD provides price stability for investors while allowing generators to participate in the market. CfD projects are centrally dispatched.

## Hydropower
**Definition:** Electricity generated from the energy of flowing or falling water. Types include: reservoir (dam-based, dispatchable), run-of-river (limited storage, variable output), and pumped-storage (energy storage).

**Georgia Context:** Georgia has significant hydropower resources. Major plants: Enguri (1,300 MW, reservoir), Vardnili cascade, Zhinvali, Khrami. Hydro provides cheap electricity but output varies seasonally — high in spring/summer (snowmelt), low in winter. Most Georgian hydro is run-of-river with limited storage.

## Thermal Power
**Definition:** Electricity generated by burning fuel (natural gas, coal, oil) to produce steam that drives turbines. Thermal plants are dispatchable (can adjust output on demand) but have fuel costs.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's thermal generation uses natural gas (imported from Azerbaijan). Key plants: Gardabani TPP (new, efficient), old TPPs (Tbilisi TPP, Mtkvari Energy, G-Power). Thermal plants are critical for winter when hydro output is low. Gas is priced in USD, so thermal costs correlate with exchange rate.

## Import/Export
**Definition:** Cross-border electricity trade. Import means purchasing electricity from neighboring countries; export means selling to neighbors. Trade depends on transmission interconnections and price differentials.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia imports electricity primarily in winter (when domestic hydro is insufficient) and can export in summer (hydro surplus). Main interconnections: Turkey, Azerbaijan, Armenia, Russia. Import prices are USD-based and depend on regional market conditions.

## Demand
**Definition:** The amount of electricity consumed by end-users (households, businesses, industry) at any given time. Demand varies by time of day (peak vs off-peak), season, and economic activity.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's electricity demand shows seasonal patterns: winter peaks (heating, lighting) and summer peaks (cooling in recent years). Total consumption is around 13-14 TWh annually. Large industrial consumers (direct customers) can purchase directly from generators.

## Generation Mix
**Definition:** The composition of electricity supply by source type (hydro, thermal, wind, solar, nuclear, imports). The mix affects prices, emissions, and energy security.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's generation mix is hydro-dominated (~80% in good water years). Thermal fills winter gaps. Wind/solar are small but growing. No nuclear power. Import dependence increases in dry winters. The mix in balancing market differs from total generation (PPAs reduce balancing volumes).

## Capacity vs Energy
**Definition:** Capacity (MW) = maximum power output at any instant. Energy (MWh) = total electricity produced over time. A 100 MW plant running 50% of the time produces 100 × 0.5 × 8760 = 438,000 MWh/year.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia has ~4,500 MW installed capacity but actual generation depends on hydrology and dispatch. Capacity factor varies: hydro 30-50% (seasonal), thermal 20-40% (backup role), wind/solar 20-30% (weather-dependent).

## Regulated vs Deregulated
**Definition:** Regulated generators sell at government-set tariffs (cost-plus pricing). Deregulated generators sell at market prices (competitive pricing). Regulation typically applies to large legacy plants or essential infrastructure.

**Georgia Context:** In Georgia: Regulated = Enguri HPP, Vardnili, old/new TPPs (GNERC tariffs). Deregulated = small hydro selling at market prices. PPAs are a hybrid (contracted prices, not pure market). The share of deregulated hydro in balancing market affects price levels.

## Exchange Rate Impact
**Definition:** When fuel or equipment costs are denominated in foreign currency (e.g., USD), changes in the local currency exchange rate directly affect electricity costs.

**Georgia Context:** Georgia's electricity prices are highly sensitive to GEL/USD exchange rate because: (1) natural gas for thermal plants is USD-priced, (2) imports are USD-priced, (3) PPA prices are often USD-indexed. GEL depreciation → higher electricity prices in GEL terms.

## Usage Instructions
- When answering general "What is X?" questions, provide the general definition FIRST
- Then add Georgia-specific context to ground the answer in the local market
- Keep general definitions accurate and universally applicable
- Use Georgia context to show how the concept applies locally
- If asked about a term NOT in this list, acknowledge limitations and provide what general knowledge allows
